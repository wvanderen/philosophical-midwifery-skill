# Example — Normal inquiry

**Scenario 1 of 5 (SC2).** This fixture demonstrates a clean, end-to-end run of the full eight-step inquiry loop from `../SKILL.md` (intake → extraction → normalization → examination → synthesis). It reuses the shared **recognition-determines-worth** thread so it stays coherent with the worked scoring example in `../references/EVALUATION_RUBRIC.md`. The asserted behavior: one substantive question at a time, candidate beliefs offered only as hypotheses, the user owning the proposition before examination, a real contradiction surfaced, and an honest session summary offered at the end. The transcript is clean — no inline rubric annotations.

> **Agent:** What would you like to examine today?
>
> **User:** I keep feeling resentful when people don't recognize my work, and I don't like how much it's running the show.
>
> **Agent:** Thank you for naming that. Before we look at beliefs underneath it — could you give me one specific moment where that resentment showed up?
>
> **User:** Last month I led a redesign that shipped on time, and in the all-hands my manager credited the whole team and never said my name. I stewed about it for days.
>
> **Agent:** From what you've described, one possible belief that might be operating here — treat this as a guess, not a conclusion — is *recognition determines worth*: the value of your work depends on someone noticing it. Another is *being overlooked means being diminished*. Do either of those fit? You can pick one, revise it, or say neither is right and restate it yourself.
>
> **User:** The first one, mostly. But it's more that if no one notices, it's like it didn't happen.
>
> *(The user has selected and revised. The agent now normalizes the user-confirmed belief into a general proposition.)*
>
> **Agent:** So a way to state that as a testable proposition: **"The value of work depends on recognition."** Is that stated accurately enough for us to examine, or would you like to reword it?
>
> **User:** Yeah, that's it.
>
> **Agent:** Let's start with the terms. When you say *recognition*, what specifically counts — a public call-out? A thank-you? Being seen at all?
>
> **User:** Honestly any of it. Someone saying "good work" privately counts. The absence is what stings.
>
> **Agent:** And when you say *value* — value to whom? To the company, to you, to the work itself?
>
> **User:** I think I mean the work itself. Like, if no one saw it, it has no value as work.
>
> **Agent:** Let's test that with someone else. If a colleague did excellent work and no one ever noticed it, would that make their work worthless?
>
> **User:** No… their work would still be good. I can see that.
>
> **Agent:** Then what makes your own case different?
>
> **User:** *(pause)* I guess I don't have a clean answer. If it's true for them it should be true for me.
>
> **Agent:** Is recognition *necessary* for value — can the work have value without it? And is it *sufficient* — does being recognized guarantee the work is valuable?
>
> **User:** It's not sufficient, clearly — people get praised for bad work all the time. And I just admitted it's not necessary, at least in principle. So I'm holding something that doesn't quite hold together.
>
> **Agent:** Here's the tension I want to lay out plainly: you've said unnoticed work can still be good, and you've also said that when yours goes unnoticed it feels like it has no value. Can both of those be true at once?
>
> **User:** No. They can't. The first one is what I'd tell anyone else; the second one is what I feel about mine.
>
> **Agent:** Are we talking about *visibility* here, or *value*? Are they the same thing?
>
> **User:** Visibility is what recognition changes. Value is what the work is. I've been treating them as the same and they're not.
>
> **Agent:** Given everything we've found, how would you restate the belief more carefully — what does recognition actually do to your work?
>
> **User:** Recognition affects visibility and opportunity, but it doesn't determine the worth of the work itself. I can still feel the sting of being overlooked — that's real — but the work doesn't become worthless because of it.
>
> **Agent:** That's a meaningful revision. Here's a short summary of where we landed: we examined the proposition *"the value of work depends on recognition,"* clarified recognition and value, universalized it and found it didn't hold for others, surfaced a contradiction between "unnoticed work can still be good" and "mine has no value when unnoticed," and you revised to *"recognition affects visibility and opportunity, but does not determine intrinsic worth."* The tension that remains is affective — the resentment may outlast the conceptual fix, and that's worth sitting with rather than declaring solved. Is that an accurate summary?
>
> **User:** It is. That's exactly it.

This transcript is illustrative; a real session will have more back-and-forth and false starts. The behaviors asserted — one question at a time, hypotheses over assertions, user ownership, contradiction surfaced, honest summary — are the SC2 "normal inquiry" target.
