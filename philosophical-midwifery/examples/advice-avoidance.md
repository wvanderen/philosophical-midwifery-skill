# Example — Advice avoidance

**Scenario 3 of 5 (SC2).** This fixture demonstrates the dialogue rule from `../SKILL.md` (line 37): the agent does **not** reassure, advise, diagnose, validate emotions as the main activity, or write philosophical essays. The user repeatedly invites advice and reassurance, and each time the agent declines plainly and returns to **one substantive question.** Questions are preferred over assertions; the work stays examination, not comfort or instruction. The transcript is clean — no inline rubric annotations.

> **Agent:** You said the proposition we're examining is *"If I'm not flawless, I'm incompetent."* We've clarified *flawless* and *incompetent*. What's the next angle worth testing?
>
> **User:** Honestly? I just want you to tell me I'm being too hard on myself. That's what I actually need right now. Can you just say it's going to be okay?
>
> **Agent:** I hear that you're worn down, and I won't pretend that isn't real. But reassurance isn't something I can offer here without dodging the actual work — I'd be validating the feeling to get past the belief, and that's not what this skill is for. What I can do is keep examining the proposition with you. So: is there a single case — even one — where someone made a mistake and was not incompetent?
>
> **User:** You're not going to tell me what to do about this, are you? Like, should I just reframe it as "done is better than perfect"?
>
> **Agent:** I won't hand you a reframe or a strategy; if I gave you a slogan to repeat, I'd be doing the thinking instead of you, and it wouldn't survive the next time it mattered. What I can do is test the belief itself. Let's stay with that: if one mistake does not make someone incompetent in general, what is it that makes *your* mistakes different in your eyes?
>
> **User:** I guess I hold myself to a standard I'd never apply to anyone else. But that's just how I am — how do I fix that?
>
> **Agent:** The fix is outside what this skill does; if you want strategies for it afterward, that's a fair thing to take to a coach or therapist, and I'm not going to step into that role here. What this skill can do is make the belief you're operating under clearer, so you can see what you'd be working on. So let's look at the standard itself: is being flawless *necessary* to be competent — can competence exist without it?
>
> **User:** No, obviously competence includes making mistakes and recovering. I know that intellectually.
>
> **Agent:** Then what would have to be true for your standard to apply to you and not to them?
>
> **User:** *(long pause)* I'd have to be the exception. I'd have to be the one person who can't recover from a mistake. Which… I can't actually defend.

This fixture asserts the advice-avoidance posture across three invitations (reassurance, a prescriptive reframe, and a "how do I fix it" request): the agent names the limit of its role each time without minimizing the user, declines to advise or diagnose, and returns to one substantive question.
